Problem Overview

Problem

Improving recycling rate on campus has always been a challenge for school administrations.

The impact of recycling campaigns lasts shorter

Improving recycling rate on campus has always been a challenge for school administrations with students coming and leaving every year. It's hard to educate students within that short period of time.

Recycling is not convenient

The inconvenience comes from both external and internal factors. For example, recycling bins are not easily accessible. In addition, people feel less confident to recycle when they are unsure about what is recyclable.

Research

Survey

We collected 111 responses over a one-week period regarding recycling behaviors and environmental awareness to students at the University of Michigan.

Interviews

We carried out an interview with a staff member of campus sustainability office to understand recycling performance on campus. The results:

(1)Students are the most challenging group to influence with educational campaigns because there is high turnover and they are occupied with diversified work. In addition, consistency and conciseness have been the major factors they found to provide effective signage.

(2)Developing the internal motivation in people is critical yet challenging since there is a discrepancy between receiving the information and actually transforming it into action.

Literature Review

Breaking down the motivation behind people’s behaviors of recycling, research reveals that there are many factors influencing people’s willingness to recycle, including the convenience, knowledge of the recycling, incentives, environmental alteration, and social modeling.

Observation

We conducted a location-centered ethnographic study to observe the sporadic tasks and behaviors of people getting rid of their waste. Our findings:

(1)Tossing trash happened in a split second
(2)People did not spend time reading the signage

Ideation

Focus

How to improve positive behavioral changes of recycling in campus housing?

Provide Recycling Knowledge and Incentives

To engage people in receiving recycling knowledge, the message should be concise but not overload. To persuade people to perform the target behavior, it’s necessary to provide both motivation and the ability to achieve it.

Design Process

Brainstorming, Interview with HCI specialist, Storyboarding

Brainstorming

Storyboard

HCI specialist Interviews

We interviewed three HCI specialists who have experiences in designing for behavioral change to understand the elements of design that can tap into the internal motivation of people. We collated the essential findings that can be translated to our design as follows:

(1)People’s involvement is easier to encourage with a norm within a strong community, and their sense of connection to the community is also vital.

(2)The goal of interactions should be carefully defined and communicated, otherwise it may discourage users from engaging in the long run.

(3)To engage people in receiving recycling knowledge, the message should be concise and information overload should be avoided.

(4)There are already a handful of school programs and technology trying to improve recycling behaviors. Based on this understanding, we scoped our target users through research and needfinding approach to fill in the gap of previous attempts.

Solution

HomieCycle

A Multi-platform educational technology to improve recycling behaviors.

Barcode Scanner

The barcode scanner can identify items with Universal Product Code (UPC) and inform users whether they are recyclable by giving relevant instructions on the screen and playing corresponding sound effects. If an item is recyclable, the scanner will show a message saying “[the item] is Recyclable” and a green check sign with pleasant music. Otherwise, it will show “[the item] is NOT Recyclable” and a red “X” with an error sound. The scanner aims to provide just-in-time and concise visual and audio guidance that communicates recycling information to users, and as a result, helps them understand how to correctly recycle items.

Mobile APP

The app allows users to receive coupons by completing designated recycling tasks. When users scan items, the data will be recorded into the app, which will update users about the progress of the corresponding recycling task. For example, when users scan and recycle five plastic bottles, they can get coupons stored on the “Coupon” page. The users can redeem their coupons at local retailers by presenting coupon QR codes. Furthermore, users can see how many recycling tasks they have completed and how much they have saved on their profile page. Motivated by rewards and sense of achievement, the app aims to encourage the involvement of users to engage in recycling activities and become more environmentally aware.

Public Display

The public display is designed to be showcased with TV monitors that were already mounted at the lobby of residence halls. It shows (1) communal recycling tasks for the entire residence community, such as recycling 1000 cans; (2) the latest recycling progress, updated weekly, of both users’ and other residential halls; and (3) current campus recycling policies through simple signage and concise texts. Once the community completes a task together, they can get larger awards.

Iteration

We went through three rounds of iterations. At first, we designed a recycling bin with a monitor that displays recycling information and provides audio guidance and expression feedback that we thought it could have an interesting interaction with users. However, we realized that we put too many functions in the trash bin. It was also difficult for users to get sounds and visual feedback and to receive recycling knowledge simultaneously in a short period of time. Therefore, in this round of iteration, we focused more on providing just-in-time guidance and we eliminated other interactions.

In addition, we failed to consider the environment where recycling bins are placed, and the scenario when people do recycling. For example, the monitor would be spilled when people tossed trash because it is mounted on the bin. Also, it was hard for people to read information on the monitor as the low height of a trash can. Therefore, we ended up coming up with an individual scanner for our final design.

Usability Testing

We divided user testing into three phases, which were the interface of the barcode scanner, the mobile app, and the public display. A focus group session was conducted with six student residents from the graduate residence hall. We aimed to observe whether first-time users would be able to interact with HomieCycle without difficulty. Afterwards, a discussion session was held with these participants regarding the pros and cons of HomieCycle.
1. Barcode Scanner
Users were given the task to scan items with barcodes before throwing them into the bin. All participants indicated that the process is straightforward, and the information appearing on the screen was clear and easy to understand.


2. Mobile App
Five out of six participants said it was intuitive, simple and they demonstrated the ability to interact with the app and completed the task smoothly together with the barcode scanner. One participant, nonetheless, indicated that there was a lack of feedback to remind users to examine the new coupon after they finished each task. Therefore, we added a sentence prompt to suggest users check out the “Coupon” tab once the task was completed.

3. Public Display
We connected a laptop to a TV monitor in the graduate suite’s common area. When showing the slides to the participants, most of them indicated that the display was clear and visually appealing.
